Accounting Calendars, Years, and Periods

Multiple accounting calendars can be defined in Singleview. An accounting calendar groups one or more accounting years.

In a Singleview implementation that requires multiple overlapping accounting years or periods. The overlapping years or periods must belong to different accounting calendars. That is, no accounting year or period belonging to the same accounting calendar can overlap.

A reporting entity references an accounting calendar to determine the accounting period to use for financial reporting.

An accounting period is a period of time for which a set of financial statements is generated. Within an accounting year, the length of the accounting period is arbitrary and fixed, based on the reporting requirements of the company (for example, day, week, month, quarter, or year).

Accounting years and accounting periods in Convergent Billing must reflect the financial years and accounting periods of the service provider; however, this restriction is not enforced by Convergent Billing.

Accounting years in Convergent Billing are normally broken down into smaller accounting periods. Start and end dates for accounting years and periods are flexible; therefore, Convergent Billing supports any type of accounting period. Examples are:

  • Calendar months
  • Calendar quarters
  • Twelve periods following a 4-4-5 week profile, ending on any day of the week (that is, a four week period, followed by a four week period, followed by a five week period).
  • Thirteen periods of four weeks.

Accounting periods are used by Convergent Billing during the:

  • Rating process, to group the effects of rating charges on GL codes.
  • GL upload process, to report the accounting period in which a set of uploaded transactions were posted to the Sales Journal.

Accounting years and periods support date and date-time granularity. Although accounting years and periods have a duration, neither entity has history records (that is, date ranging is not supported and modifications are made directly on the entity).

Accounting years and periods have a status of Open or Closed. After a period is closed, journal entries cannot be added to the period; the journal entries are reflected in the current period. An accounting year is automatically closed if all of its accounting periods are closed.

More than one accounting period can be open at any one time, in which case a journal entry is posted to the period to which it logically belongs.
